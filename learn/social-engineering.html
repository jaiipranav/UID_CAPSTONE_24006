Social Engineering

Social engineering is the art of psychologically manipulating people into performing actions or divulging confidential information. Unlike technical hacks that exploit software vulnerabilities, social engineering targets human psychology, exploiting trust, curiosity, and fear.

Common Techniques

  • Baiting: Luring victims with a false promise (e.g., a free movie download, a USB stick left in a public place) to trick them into installing malware or sharing credentials.
  • Pretexting: The attacker creates a fabricated scenario (a pretext) to obtain sensitive information. For example, pretending to be from IT support to get a user's password for "system maintenance".
  • Tailgating: An attacker, without proper authentication, follows an authorized person into a restricted physical area.
  • Quid Pro Quo: The attacker offers a benefit in exchange for information, which may seem harmless but can be used in a larger attack.

How to Defend Against Social Engineering

  • Be skeptical of unsolicited communications. Always verify the source through a separate, trusted channel.
  • Do not click on suspicious links or download unknown attachments.
  • Avoid sharing sensitive personal or financial information online or over the phone.
  • Be wary of any request that invokes a strong sense of urgency, fear, or curiosity.
